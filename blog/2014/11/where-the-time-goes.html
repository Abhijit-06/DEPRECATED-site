Amdahl's Law says that the speedup you can get by parallelizing a computation is limited by how much of the computational can't be sped up. For example, if 10% of a program's run time is inherently sequential, then even if you have an infinite number of processors, you can't speed it up more than 10 times.

The same rule applies to organizations like Software Carpentry. We now have almost 200 certified instructors; even working in pairs, and each teaching only once a year, that's enough to run two workshops a week, and we're training more all the time. But someone has to train them, and match them with workshops, and design new templates for lessons, and talk to potential sponsors, and those central activities are now constraining what we can accomplish.

To give you some idea of what goes on behind the scenes, here's what my typical week looks like:

Activity Hours Notes
Board discussions 11.0 Drawing up by-laws, hammering out a funding model, etc.
Other email 10.0 Questions about teaching, managing assessment, extracting Git history, etc.
Instructor training 8.0 Real-time contact hours, demo teaching, and email
Discussions with potential partners and funders 6.0 Some live, some by email
Writing/reviewing grant proposals 5.5 Some for us, some for partners
Writing and revising blog posts 4.0 Like this one
Coordinating workshops 3.0 Arliss Collins and Giacomo Peru handle most of it, but there are always things left over
Record keeping 2.5 Managing our list of who taught where, who's signed up for instructor training, etc.
Reviewing pull requests 2.5 Should be a lot more
Research projects 2.0 The code review study, the language comparison study, etc.
Technical support 2.0 Git issues, mailing list issues, web site issues, etc.
Writing letters of reference 1.0 Saying nice things about our instructors is the least I can do to thank them

That's 57.5 hours in a week that doesn't include any travel or teaching, and in which I didn't contribute to extracting history from our existing lesson repository, building a tool to check that new lessons conform to our style guide, follow up on experiments with a better build system for lessons, complete the ethics training needed for the language comparison study, or help figure out how we're going to handle translations of our lessons. It's too much to sustain long-term, particularly now that I'm doing it pro bono. More importantly, things that are vital to our long-term health and growth aren't being done, and can't be done until there are more staff.

(And no, "get volunteers to do it" isn't a solution. Many of these activities are administrative, have long lead times, and require sustained attention over a period of months; our volunteers do a great job of delivering and improving lessons, but we need full-time staff to provide continuity.)

We therefore need to raise money, both to pay my salary and to hire at least one more full-time person to share the load. We also need to partner with more institutions who can provide staff for coordinating workshops in the way that Arliss and Giacomo have been doing. While individual donations will help and are very welcome, it's vital that we build institutional ties as well.

We can offer several things in return: an agreed number of workshops each year, on-site instructor training, and help with recruiting and publicity for sponsor activities. Sponsors will also have a say in our direction: the interim board is still working out the details, but sponsors will be represented on our board, and will be able to shape what we do and how we do it. If this sounds like something that you or your organization would be interested in, please get in touch.
